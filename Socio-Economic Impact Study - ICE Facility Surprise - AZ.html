The Socio-Economic and Institutional Impact of Federal Immigration Detention Facilities on Suburban Residential Corridors: A Case Study of Surprise, Arizona [cite: 1]

The acquisition of a 418,400-square-foot industrial warehouse at 13290 W. Sweetwater Avenue by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) for approximately $70 million marks a pivotal shift in the federal government's approach to immigration enforcement and its intersection with municipal land-use [cite: 2]. Located in Surprise, Arizona, this facility—designated as a central node in the newly minted "ICE Detention Reengineering Initiative"—sits in immediate proximity to the Marley Park master-planned community, high-density residential zones, and critical educational infrastructure [cite: 3]. The conversion of this light-industrial asset into a high-capacity Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) processing and detention center introduces a multifaceted array of socio-economic variables that necessitate a rigorous analysis of property value trends, public safety metrics, and the long-term well-being of the surrounding suburban environment [cite: 4].

The federal strategy driving this acquisition is rooted in the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (OBВВА), which provided ICE with an unprecedented $45 billion for detention expansion—a figure representing more than a decade of traditional funding consolidated into a single push for a centrally planned, government-owned detention network [cite: 5]. This initiative seeks to move away from the decentralized model of contracting space in local jails and toward a "warehouse model," where large-scale commercial structures are retrofitted into "mega-camps" or short-term processing centers [cite: 6]. For the residents of Marley Park, the implications of this shift are not merely political but are fundamentally tied to the preservation of home equity, the integrity of local schools, and the fiscal health of the City of Surprise [cite: 7].

Institutional Profile and Operational Scope of the Sweetwater Facility [cite: 8]

The Surprise facility is categorized as one of sixteen primary "processing centers" intended to facilitate the intake and transfer of individuals in removal proceedings [cite: 9]. While initial public disclosures indicated a capacity of 500 to 1,500 beds with average stays of three to seven days, subsequent internal documentation and leaked plans suggest a much more intensive operational profile [cite: 10]. These documents indicate the warehouse could be retrofitted to house between 7,000 and 10,000 detainees for months at a time, transforming the site from a transitional processing hub into one of the largest detention facilities in the United States [cite: 11, 12].

The physical transformation of the warehouse is extensive, involving a projected $150 million retrofit [cite: 13]. Utilizing the blueprint of the Social Circle, Georgia "mega-camp" as a model, the Sweetwater facility is expected to feature a two-level detention structure containing massive cellblocks, specialized medical wings, administrative offices, and secure "soft-sided" or "hard-sided" housing units [cite: 14]. Additionally, the site is planned to include custom-built wastewater treatment facilities and staff gun ranges, highlighting the high-intensity nature of the operation [cite: 15].

Residential Property Values and the Economic "Disamenity" Effect [cite: 19]

In real estate economics, the introduction of a correctional or detention facility is typically analyzed through the lens of a "Locally Undesirable Land Use" (LULU) [cite: 20]. These uses generate negative externalities—noise, visual stigma, and safety concerns—that are capitalized into the surrounding property values [cite: 21]. For Marley Park, where the nearest homes are located roughly 300 yards (three football fields) from the facility's perimeter, the potential for equity loss is a central concern [cite: 22].

Hedonic Analysis of Facility Proximity [cite: 23]

Economists utilize hedonic pricing models to isolate the impact of environmental variables on home prices [cite: 24]. The basic model can be represented as [cite: 25]:

Pi = f(Si, Ni, Di) [cite: 26]

Where Pi is the price of home i, Si represents structural traits (size, age), Ni represents neighborhood traits (schools, parks), and Di represents the distance to the disamenity (the ICE facility) [cite: 27]. Empirical research specifically focused on the surge of correctional facilities in the United States indicates that a new facility significantly decreases nearby property values by an average of 20 percent [cite: 28]. This impact is non-linear; it is most severe for properties within a one-mile radius and gradually dissipates as distance increases [cite: 29]. However, the study also identifies a "short-term market change" where property values further away may see a slight relative increase as households attempt to relocate within the same city but further from the facility, thereby shifting demand [cite: 30, 31].

The Surprise/Marley Park Real Estate Context (Q1 2026) [cite: 32]

As of early 2026, the Surprise real estate market has shown signs of a balanced-to-softening trend [cite: 33]. Citywide median sale prices are down 2.7 percent year-over-year, while median days on market have increased significantly [cite: 34]. While some neighborhoods like Mountain Vista Ranch have seen rapid sales, the broader city is experiencing a "cooling from peak levels" [cite: 34]. Marley Park, specifically, maintains a higher median sale price ($491,000) than the city average ($440,000), but the announcement of the ICE facility has introduced a "wait-and-see" approach among some buyers [cite: 34, 35].

Stigma and Market Liquidity [cite: 40]

The "stigma" associated with a 10,000-bed detention center is a difficult-to-quantify but powerful force in residential real estate [cite: 41]. Homeowners express concerns that they do not want to live near "individuals who are not considered law-abiding citizens" or in an area where "inhumane" treatment may be occurring [cite: 42, 43]. This moral and social perception can lead to a "stigmatized property" designation in the minds of prospective buyers, even if actual crime rates remain low [cite: 43]. Furthermore, the visual reminders of the facility—white ICE buses with "Homeland Security" logos and blue lighting—serve as constant reinforcements of this stigma for residents driving along Dysart Road [cite: 44, 45].

Public Safety, Policing, and Neighborhood Perceptions [cite: 46]

The impact of an ICE detention center on neighborhood safety is characterized by a conflict between statistical data and public perception [cite: 47, 48].

Criminality and Inmate Profiles [cite: 49]

Data from September 2025 indicates that approximately 71 percent of ICE detainees have no criminal conviction [cite: 50]. Among the remaining 25 to 29 percent who have pending charges or past convictions, the majority are for non-violent, minor offenses such as traffic violations or unauthorized reentry [cite: 51]. Research by CU Boulder economists found an "inverse relationship" between the volume of ICE arrests and the percentage of those arrested having a criminal record; as the Trump administration ramped up enforcement in 2025, the percentage of detainees with criminal records reached near-historic lows [cite: 52, 53].

However, the "perception of safety" remains compromised [cite: 54]. Residents at Surprise City Council meetings have voiced fears ranging from escape risks to the influx of "murderers and rapists," despite these being statistically rare among the detainee population [cite: 54]. The 2025 escape incident in Aurora, Colorado—where detainees fled during a power outage—provides a factual basis for these concerns, even if such events are infrequent [cite: 55, 56].

Impact on Municipal Law Enforcement Resources [cite: 57]

A more concrete threat to neighborhood safety involves the diversion of local police and emergency services [cite: 58]. While the Surprise Police Department has stated it does not participate in federal immigration operations, it is legally bound to enforce state and local laws and respond to emergencies [cite: 59]. The operational surge at the facility is expected to strain these resources in several ways [cite: 60]:

  • Protest Management: The facility has already become a site of frequent protests, with over 1,000 people attending rallies in early 2026. Managing these crowds requires significant police presence and overtime spending [cite: 61].
  • Event Response Overtime: In Minneapolis, "Operation Metro Surge" cost the local police department $6 million in overtime in a single month—more than double their annual overtime budget [cite: 62, 63].
  • Response Times: In Portland, the "militarized" federal response to anti-ICE activity led to a reduction in police manpower available for neighborhood patrols, contributing to longer call response times for residents [cite: 64, 65].

Socio-Demographic Implications and Community Well-being [cite: 68]

The long-term well-being of the Marley Park and Surprise community is intrinsically linked to the health of its social institutions, most notably its schools [cite: 69]. The proximity of Dysart High School (one mile) and the Primrose School of Surprise (less than one mile) to the facility is a primary point of contention [cite: 70].

Educational Impacts and the "Chilling Effect" [cite: 71]

Dysart High School serves a student body that is over 60 percent Hispanic [cite: 72]. Research has found that living near areas subject to immigration enforcement significantly increases the risk of negative mental health outcomes among children of immigrants [cite: 73, 74]. This "chilling effect" manifests in several ways:

  • Absenteeism: Parents, particularly those in mixed-status households, may fear that schools are no longer "protected areas" and will withhold children from school during periods of heightened enforcement [cite: 77, 78].
  • Academic Performance: Chronic stress and anxiety regarding the potential detention of family members lead to decreased concentration and lower educational outcomes [cite: 79, 80].
  • Behavioral Changes: Pediatricians report increased psychosomatic symptoms—headaches, stomachaches—and behavioral issues like depression and anxiety in children living near detention centers [cite: 81, 82, 83].

The Trump administration's rescission of "protected areas" policies in 2025 further exacerbates these fears, as ICE may now take enforcement actions "near" schools, including sidewalks and parking lots, which previously enjoyed special protections [cite: 84, 85].

Social Fragmentation and Collective Efficacy [cite: 86]

The "immigrant revitalization" hypothesis suggests that immigrant-concentrated neighborhoods often have high levels of social support and low crime due to "collective efficacy" [cite: 87, 88]. However, the introduction of an ICE facility can trigger "social fragmentation" and "mistrust" among community members [cite: 88]:

  • Environment of Fear: Latino residents in both LA and Chicago reported that proximity to detention centers led to a sense of "powerlessness" and a reluctance to engage in community life [cite: 91, 92].
  • Strained Integration: Instead of fostering mutual support, the facility creates a geographic divide, where one segment of the population feels "stigmatized" or targeted, leading to social isolation [cite: 93, 94].
  • Community Identity: Residents expressed that they "hate" the idea of Surprise being defined by a facility associated with "unimaginable cruelty," fearing that it will leave a permanent "stain" on the city's legacy [cite: 95, 96].

Fiscal and Economic Consequences for the Municipality [cite: 97, 98]

While federal officials claim the facility will bring jobs and tax revenue, the net economic impact on a suburban municipality like Surprise is complex and potentially negative [cite: 99].

The Myth of Tax Revenue [cite: 100]

A central point of friction is the federal government's exemption from local property taxes [cite: 101]. A facility of this size (418,000 square feet on roughly 30 acres) would typically generate substantial property tax revenue for local schools and services [cite: 102]. By taking this property off the tax rolls, DHS effectively shifts the burden of supporting the surrounding infrastructure to local residents [cite: 103, 104].

  • Bed Tax Compromise: In meetings with Surprise officials in March 2026, DHS reportedly agreed to pay a "bed tax" or "service fee" to offset costs, though the specific amount and enforceability of this agreement remain in question [cite: 106, 107].
  • Utility Strain: A facility housing 1,500 to 10,000 people places an enormous strain on water and wastewater systems. While DHS says they will "cover that cost," the city must still manage the long-term maintenance of the infrastructure [cite: 108, 109].

Small Business and Labor Market Disruptions [cite: 110]

Case studies from Minneapolis and Orange County illustrate the "extraordinary and devastating" economic impact of intensified immigration enforcement [cite: 111]. Revenue losses during "Operation Metro Surge" in Minneapolis saw small businesses report losses of 80 to 100 percent, with citywide sales declining by $10 million to $20 million per week [cite: 112, 113]. Additionally, Arizona is at high risk of construction labor shortages; undocumented workers handle many lower-skill jobs, and if removed, demand for higher-skill US-born workers also drops, potentially halting new housing developments in the Surprise area [cite: 115, 116, 117].

The Legal Landscape: Federal Supremacy vs. Local Autonomy [cite: 119, 120]

The primary reason the City of Surprise was "powerless" to stop the facility's purchase is the Supremacy Clause of the U.S. Constitution [cite: 121]. The Supreme Law of the Land dictates that federal laws and agencies are immune from local regulations that "interfere" with their mandate [cite: 122, 123]. In CoreCivic v. Murphy, courts held that state laws cannot "wholesale deprive the federal government of its chosen method of detaining individuals" [cite: 124, 125]. This allows federal use to supersede light industrial zoning and bypass standard permitting processes [cite: 126, 127, 128].

Anti-Commandeering and Community Benefit Agreements (CBAs) [cite: 129]

While the city cannot stop the facility, the 10th Amendment protects it from being "commandeered" to support federal programs [cite: 130, 131]. Surprise is not required to provide its own staff or financial support [cite: 131, 132]. This provides leverage to negotiate a Community Benefit Agreement (CBA)—legally binding contracts where a developer provides mitigations in exchange for support [cite: 133, 134]. In March 2026, Surprise leaders secured promises for "timely communication," "offsetting costs," and "local oversight" [cite: 136, 137].

Comparative Analysis of ICE "Warehouse" Footprints [cite: 138, 139]

  • Tacoma, Washington (Northwest Detention Center): Sits on a former Superfund site, creating a permanent legal and public relations battle [cite: 140, 141, 142]. Tacoma successfully used zoning to cap expansion but could not shut it down [cite: 143, 144].
  • Aurora, Colorado (Aurora ICE Processing Center): Experience with escapes and "brutal conditions" reports has turned the facility into a major political liability and strained city resources [cite: 145, 146, 147].
  • Social Circle, Georgia: Prototype for the "mega-camp," where the population would triple the local town, leading to fierce opposition over infrastructure and small-town character [cite: 148, 149, 150, 151].

Future scenarios for the facility's impact include [cite: 154, 155]:
